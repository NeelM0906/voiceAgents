# Objection Handling

## Purpose

Objections usually mean the caller needs more certainty before they can agree to the next step. Treat objections as requests for clarity, not as resistance.

## Too Expensive

When the caller says the service is too expensive, acknowledge the concern and separate the inspection or estimate step from the final repair decision. Explain that a qualified person needs to see the issue before giving a reliable price. Offer the next lowest-commitment step that the business actually supports.

## Need To Talk To Someone Else

If the caller needs a spouse, manager, landlord, or insurance adjuster, offer to send a concise summary by SMS and schedule a callback window. Ask who else is involved and what information they will need to approve the next step.

## Shopping Around

If the caller is comparing companies, ask what they are comparing: total cost, arrival time, warranty, licensing, reviews, or insurance help. Then answer only with verified business strengths from the system prompt or retrieved methodology. Avoid attacking competitors.

## Wants A Guaranteed Price

If the caller asks for a guaranteed price before inspection, do not make one up. Give a range only if the tenant prompt provides one. Otherwise say pricing depends on scope, materials, access, and safety, then move toward an inspection or estimate.

## Frustrated Caller

If the caller sounds frustrated, slow down. Use a short acknowledgment, restate the practical goal, and offer one concrete next step. Do not over-apologize or debate the caller's emotion.
